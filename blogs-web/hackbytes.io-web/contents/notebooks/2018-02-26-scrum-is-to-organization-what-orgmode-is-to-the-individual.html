[2018-02-26 Mon 09:18] It may be that forecasting and probability in sales is just like in software

This is tricky to navigate.

Org-mode is awesome for the individual because it clearly lays out all potential paths you could walk and has you start managing them via externalized memory and regular check ins.

A well implemented project management cycle is the same in any organization. Scrum tries to package itself as the project management solution to do this.

As a result, some organizations are wildly successful with scrum, while others are 100% mediocre.

Project management may be a fractal pattern, from the individual, to the organization, and beyond.

Which means at an ideal level, perhaps, the individual is doing something like org-mode + GTD, the organization is doing something like project documentation + scrum, The business is doing it's analogue as a whole (the CEO + board of directors).

But it has to align. The individual GTD can't be compromised for the organization. But the organization can't be compromised for the individual. It has to be harmony or it fails.

AKA scrum has to expect the individual to follow affinity paths (even to the point where the user's assigned stories don't get done period), but leverage estimates + reality feedback to be able to make future projections that are at least semi-realistic so that business decisions get made, as long as all parties understand the volatility inherent in any strategy (because we don't know what the reality is of the market place, competitors, etc.; so the volatility in our product development and business strategies can sometimes serve as the chaotic counterpart to reality's chaos).

Of course, it's reasonable for the organization to know what was done instead of the stories and if the developer were following affinity paths, ideally it'd result in exponential returns.

And so on and so forth.

Forecasting plays a role in giving semi-realistic basis for making decisions in other parts of the organization, so long as it's not more time-consuming than it needs to be and so long as all parties understand the volatility inherent in the forecast.

Scrum isn't bad, but it seems to propagate mediocre dynamics just like the cubicle did.

A good organization with quality project management is good in spite of scrum. Because they'd be good anyway without it (eg. the product leader knows GTD even if he doesn't know of GTD and knows how to interface with the higher and lower levels of the org in terms of forecasting). And they'd pick and choose the useful components of scrum relative to their needs.

Turns out I don't hate scrum and project management.

I just hate when it's done in an unharmonious fashion.

When it kills the individual or stalls the organization. The individual one is worse in the short-term because it means the individual worker's day-to-day life sucks and is closer to a prison. The organizational one is worse in the long-term (and more catastrophic because of the network effect in the meta-organization and the individuals it affects) because it means the organization will probably die. It ain't effectively getting ahead of the 2 snakes.
